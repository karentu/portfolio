Background

Every single day, 1 in 4 patients who checked into a waiting room in the US waited longer to see a physician than triage standard guidelines.

Emergency Department overcrowding in the US. Between the years of 1997 and 2006, EDs saw a volume increase of 32% while the total number of EDs actually decreased by 186. 91% of ED directors classified their own EDs as overcrowded. THat is, waiting rooms were full more than 6 hours per day and acutely ill patients waiting longer than 60 minutes to see a physician.

The consequence? Patients falling through the cracks, resulting in more serious sometimes fatal prognoses. My team decided to focus on developing a system for continuous patient monitoring in order to decrease the load on already overworked triage nurses and secure better patient monitoring in the hours before they see a doctor.

Design Process

Out team spent over 70 hours shadowing ED physicians and nurses in order to determine the primary pain points and design challenges of the space.

Our first round of brainstorming involved exploring a number of different approaches to patient monitoring, from restructuring the ER architecturally to wi-fi vitals monitoring technology and mobile tracking apps.

We finaly decided to pursue vitals monitoring as it would be the most easy to adopt in a variety of different hospital systems and was the least intrusive to patient. We then prototyped several different designs using cardboard and various other materials in order to determine the physical feasibility of various designs.

Each of the designs was evaluated based on the following design considerations:

  • Integration in Existing Ecosystem
  • Patient Comfort
  • Caretaker Usability
  • Wear and breakage
  • Theft
  • Data security
  • Accuracy
  • Sterilization
